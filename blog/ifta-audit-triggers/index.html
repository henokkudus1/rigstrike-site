What Triggers an IFTA Audit (and How to Survive One)

By RigStrike · July 10, 2026

An audit letter is not the end of the world. Every state audits a slice of its IFTA filers each year, and plenty of those audits close clean with no money changing hands. The drivers who sweat are the ones whose paperwork does not back up their numbers. So let us talk about what actually gets you flagged, and what keeps you calm when the envelope shows up.

How they pick who to look at

Some audits are just the luck of the draw. States are required to audit a share of their filers, so a chunk of it is random. The rest is pattern matching. Auditors run your returns through checks, and when something looks off compared to how a real truck behaves, your file gets a second look. None of the triggers below are about being a bad guy. They are about numbers that do not line up.

The triggers that get you a second look

Miles that do not reconcile. Your reported jurisdiction miles should match your GPS or ELD trail and your odometer. If you say 1,800 miles in a state but your route and your total odometer movement do not support it, that is a flag. Total miles that do not add up to the distance between your fuel stops is another.

Fuel receipts that do not match. The gallons you claim you bought in a state have to be backed by receipts that show the date, seller, location, and gallons. Claiming more gallons than your receipts prove, or leaning on receipts that are missing details, invites questions. So does buying fuel in a state you supposedly never drove through.

Stale rates. Rates change quarter to quarter. File Q2 numbers using last quarter's rates and your tax comes out wrong. It is an honest mistake, but it makes your whole return look sloppy. Always pull the rate for the quarter you are filing. The calculator loads the current matrix so you are not copying an old chart.

Rounded or estimated jurisdiction miles. If every state on your return ends in a clean hundred, an auditor knows you eyeballed it. Real trips do not come out round. Estimated miles are one of the most common findings, and they usually mean the auditor rebuilds your mileage from scratch, their way.

Late or missing zero reports. A skipped quarter, even one where you ran nothing, is a bright flag. It tells the state your record keeping has holes. File every quarter while your license is active, zero or not. Our step-by-step filing guide covers the zero-report rule in full.

MPG that looks impossible. Auditors know what a loaded truck gets. A fleet MPG of 9 or 10 on a heavy rig, or a number that swings wildly from quarter to quarter, or the exact same MPG four quarters in a row, all read as made-up. Your MPG should look like a real truck working real loads.

Here is what "does not reconcile" looks like in practice. Say you report 600 miles through a state but you bought no fuel there and your route only threads the corner of it for what should be about 40 miles. Or you report a fleet MPG of 5.0 one quarter and 8.5 the next with the same truck and the same lanes. Each of those is a number that a real truck would not produce, and each one is easy for software to catch. The fix is never to guess. Report what your records actually show, even when it is not tidy.

The records that clear you

Surviving an audit is almost entirely about records. If your numbers came from real, contemporaneous records, the audit is a formality. Keep these for every truck:

  • Trip records with the date, the route, total miles, and miles broken out by jurisdiction, plus your beginning and ending odometer. GPS or ELD data is the gold standard here because it is created as you drive, not reconstructed at the quarter's end.
  • Fuel records for every purchase: date, seller name and location, gallons, price, fuel type, and the unit number of the truck it went into. Keep the actual receipts, not just a summary.
  • A distance source you can point to. Whether it is an ELD, a GPS tracker, or trip sheets, you want one consistent method that produces the same miles the auditor would get.

The through-line is simple. Your return should be a summary of records that already exist, not a set of numbers you built to fill in the form.

How long to keep everything

IFTA record retention runs four years from the return's due date or filing date. That is the standard across member jurisdictions, though your base state can have its own wrinkles, so confirm with them. Practically, keep four full years of trip and fuel records per truck, backed up somewhere that will not get coffee spilled on it. Digital copies are fine and a lot easier to hand over than a shoebox.

A five-minute check before you file

Run this quick pass on every return before you submit it, and most triggers never get a chance to fire:

  • Do your total miles roughly match the distance between your fuel stops for the quarter?
  • Does every state with reported miles make sense for the routes you actually ran?
  • Are your jurisdiction miles the real numbers, not rounded to clean hundreds?
  • Does every gallon you claimed have a receipt with date, location, and gallons on it?
  • Did you use the rates for the quarter you are filing, not an older chart?
  • Does your fleet MPG look like a working truck, and is it in the same neighborhood as last quarter?

A "no" on any line is not a disaster. It is a chance to fix the number before the state sees it. That is the whole game: catch your own soft spots first.

If the letter shows up

Do not panic and do not ignore it. Read what they are asking for and the window you have to respond. Pull the quarters in question, gather the trip and fuel records that back them, and be consistent. If you find an honest error while you prep, that is normal. Auditors see mistakes all day. What they are really testing is whether your records are real and your method is sound. If both hold up, you are fine.

One thing worth understanding: auditors often sample. They may pick a few months, check how closely your records match your filed numbers, and apply what they find across the whole period under review. That cuts both ways. Tight records in the sample work in your favor everywhere. Sloppy records in the sample can get projected across quarters you thought were fine. It is one more reason the every-trip habit beats the quarter-end scramble. New to filing and want to start clean? Our step-by-step guide walks the whole process.

The best audit defense is the boring one: clean records, captured as you go, using the same method every quarter. Get that right and an audit is just paperwork.

IFTA by Hand vs an App vs an Accountant

By RigStrike · July 10, 2026

There are three ways to get your IFTA filed. Do it yourself, pay somebody to do it, or let software do the grunt work. None of them is wrong. They just trade your time for your money in different amounts. Here is the honest version, so you can pick the one that actually fits how you run.

By hand: cheap in dollars, expensive in hours

Filing yourself costs nothing but your time, and it costs a lot of that. You sort a quarter of fuel receipts, total your miles by state, look up the right rate for each jurisdiction, run the fuel-tax math, and key it all into your base state's portal. Drivers who do this well commonly report spending a few hours per quarter once the records are gathered, and longer than that if the shoebox is a mess.

The dollar cost is zero, but there are two hidden ones. First, the hours are yours, usually on a night you would rather be resting. Second, hand math is where mistakes happen: a rate copied from last quarter, miles rounded to a clean number, a receipt that went missing. Those mistakes are exactly the ones that draw a second look, which we cover in what triggers an IFTA audit.

Doing it by hand makes the most sense when you run one truck, keep tidy records, and are comfortable in a spreadsheet. A free tool takes most of the pain out of the math part. Our IFTA calculator does every state at once on the official rates, so at least the arithmetic is off your plate even if you file the rest yourself.

An accountant or service: buy back your time

Plenty of owner-operators hand IFTA to an accountant or a permit-and-tax service. You still gather the records, but they do the sorting, the math, and the filing. Pricing varies a lot. Some charge a flat fee per quarter, some roll IFTA into a monthly bookkeeping or compliance package, and some bill by the year. What you pay depends on the preparer, your number of trucks, and how clean your records are when they land on their desk. Messy records cost more, every time.

The value here is real if numbers are not your thing or you simply do not have the hours. You are buying peace of mind and somebody else's attention to the deadlines. The catch is that an accountant can only work with what you hand them. If your mileage and fuel records are thin, they cannot fix that, and you are paying a professional rate to chase paperwork you could have captured automatically.

An app: automate the part that hurts

The pain in IFTA is not really the form. It is the tracking. Where were you, how many miles in each state, which receipts go with which quarter. An app attacks that directly. It logs your miles by state as you drive, keeps your fuel purchases in one place, and does the tax math on current rates, so filing becomes a review instead of a reconstruction.

Apps usually run on a subscription, monthly or yearly, and the honest trade is a steady small cost in exchange for getting your evenings back and cutting down on errors. For a single truck the math is about time saved and mistakes avoided. For a small fleet, automatic per-state tracking across several trucks is where it really earns its keep, because doing that by hand for multiple units is a genuine headache.

The other quiet benefit is consistency. Because an app captures your miles and fuel the same way every day, your returns line up quarter to quarter, and that consistency is exactly what keeps you off an auditor's radar. A hand filer's numbers can wander depending on how tired they were the night they did the math. Software does it the same way every time, which is boring in the best possible way.

How to choose

Three quick questions sort most people:

  • How many trucks? One truck with clean records can file by hand or with a free calculator. Multiple trucks lean hard toward an app.
  • How are your records? If your miles and receipts are already tidy, any option works. If they are a scramble at quarter's end, automatic tracking pays for itself in stress alone.
  • What is your time worth? If a few hours a quarter is fine, do it yourself. If those hours cost you loads or sleep, buy them back with a service or an app.

There is no shame in any lane. A careful owner-operator with a spreadsheet and the free calculator can file a clean return for years. The question is just where you want to spend, in dollars or in hours.

The hidden cost nobody prices in

When people compare these three, they compare the obvious costs: your hours, the accountant's fee, the app's subscription. The cost that never makes the list is the cost of getting it wrong. A stale rate, a rounded state, a missing receipt. Any of those can turn a quiet quarter into a corrected return, a penalty, or an audit that eats a day you did not have. That risk is highest with hand filing, because that is where the small mistakes hide, and it is lowest when your records are captured automatically as you drive. Whatever option you pick, price that risk in, because it is real even though it never shows up on an invoice.

A gut check on the trade

Strip it down and the choice is time versus money versus risk. By hand you spend time and carry more risk to save money. An accountant spends your money to save your time, but leans on the records you hand over. An app spends a little money to save time and cut risk by fixing the tracking at the source. There is no universal winner. There is only the version that matches your trucks, your records, and what an evening of your time is worth to you. Be honest about all three and the answer usually picks itself.

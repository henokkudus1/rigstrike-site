Where to Buy Diesel: The IFTA Math Most Truckers Get Backwards

By RigStrike · July 10, 2026

You pull into the island, look at the price on the pole, and buy where it is cheapest. Feels obvious. It is also wrong, or at least it is measuring the wrong thing. Once you file IFTA, the tax stamped into that pump price does not stay where you paid it. Understand that, and you will start reading fuel prices the way the tax man does.

Why the tax washes out

Remember how IFTA works. You owe tax on the fuel you burned in each state, based on your miles there. You get a credit for the tax already inside the fuel you bought in each state. At the end of the quarter those two settle against each other.

Follow the tax dollar through. When you buy fuel in a state, you pay that state's tax at the pump. Then on your return, that exact tax comes back to you as a credit. Meanwhile you still owe tax on the miles you drove there, whether you fueled there or not. Add it all up and the pump tax you paid cancels against the pump tax you get credited. What you are truly left paying is the fuel itself, plus the fixed tax on the miles you drove. That mileage tax is set by your route. You cannot shop your way out of it.

So the tax portion of the sticker is a wash. It is noise. The only part of the pump price that actually changes your total cost is the fuel underneath the tax.

The one subtraction that matters

Here is the whole trick in one line:

Base price = pump price minus that state's diesel tax rate.

Buy where the base price is lowest, not where the sticker is lowest. A state with a high tax rate can show a scary number on the pole and still be the cheaper fill, because more of that number is tax that is going to wash out anyway. Strip the tax off both and compare what is left.

A worked example that flips the answer

Take two neighbors, Michigan and Indiana. These diesel rates are the real Q2 2026 figures from the official IFTA matrix. The pump prices are example numbers to show the math, not live prices.

The sticker says Michigan wins by 5.0 cents a gallon. But strip out each state's tax and Indiana is the cheaper base price by 5.6 cents a gallon ($3.4190 versus $3.4750). The higher pump price in Indiana is mostly its bigger tax, and that tax washes out on your return.

On a 150 gallon fill, buying in Indiana saves you about $8.40 in true cost over Michigan. Go by the sticker and you would have driven to the wrong pump and felt good about it.

That is the backwards part. The pump you feel good about is not always the pump that costs you less once IFTA does its thing. The bigger the gap in state tax rates, the more the sticker can fool you.

"But I get the tax back anyway, so who cares?"

Fair pushback, and here is the answer, walked all the way through. When you buy in a state, you pay its tax at the pump and that tax comes back to you as an IFTA credit. So yes, you get it back. But you also owe tax on the miles you burned there, credit or no credit. Line those up and the pump tax you paid is canceled by the pump tax you are credited. The tax you truly carry is the tax on your miles, and that is fixed by where you drove, not where you fueled.

Strip the tax out of both sides and what is left is the fuel itself. Your total cost for the quarter comes down to the base price of every gallon you bought, plus that fixed mileage tax you cannot change. Minimize the base price and you minimize the bill. That is the whole reason base price is the number to shop, and sticker is the number to ignore.

It adds up faster than it looks. Five or six cents a gallon may sound like nothing, but across a couple hundred gallons a week, week after week, it is a real slice of your fuel budget. And it is money you either keep or hand over based on a single subtraction you can do in your head at the island.

The Kentucky and Virginia exception

Two states play by a slightly different rule, and they are worth knowing. Kentucky and Virginia charge a diesel surcharge on top of the base rate. For Q2 2026 that is $0.1050 per gallon in Kentucky and $0.1430 in Virginia.

The surcharge is billed on the fuel you burn in that state, and there is no credit for buying fuel there. In plain terms, you pay it on your miles through Kentucky or Virginia no matter where you fill up. You cannot buy your way out of it, and buying there does not earn it back. So the surcharge is a cost of your route, not a reason to fuel in that state or to avoid it.

For the buying decision, treat Kentucky and Virginia like everyone else and compare base price, which is pump price minus their base rate only. The surcharge is not part of the pump price and does not get credited, so it does not belong in the where-to-buy comparison. Just know it is coming on your return for the miles you ran there.

What this looks like at the island

You do not have to run four-decimal math at every stop. The habit is what matters. Train your eye to knock each state's tax off the sticker before you compare, and stop trusting the pole price on its own. Over a year of fills, choosing by base price instead of sticker is real money, and it is money you are leaving on the table right now if you buy by the pole.

A couple of honest caveats so you use this right. Base price answers one question: which pump is cheaper for an IFTA filer. It does not tell you to drive out of route to reach it. Ten extra miles for a cheaper base price can wipe out the savings in fuel and time, so the winner still has to be roughly on your line. And base price does not account for your fuel card's discounts, cash prices, or loyalty deals, which stack on top. Do the tax subtraction first to see the true ranking, then let your card discount sweeten whichever pump already won.

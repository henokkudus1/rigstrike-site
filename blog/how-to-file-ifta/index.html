IFTA basics

How to File IFTA, Step by Step (2026)

By RigStrike · July 10, 2026

The quarter ends, and there it is again. A pile of fuel receipts, a trip log that may or may not add up, and a form that wants a number for every state you touched. IFTA is not hard once you see how the pieces fit. It is just fiddly, and fiddly is where mistakes live. Here is the whole thing, start to finish.

First: do you even have to file?

IFTA is the International Fuel Tax Agreement. It exists so you file one fuel-tax return with your home state instead of a separate return for every state you drove through. You are in the club if you run a qualified motor vehicle across two or more member jurisdictions.

A qualified motor vehicle is one built and used to haul people or property that meets any one of these:

  • Has two axles and a gross vehicle weight over 26,000 pounds, or
  • Has three or more axles on the power unit no matter the weight, or
  • Is used in a combination that tips over 26,000 pounds gross.

If that is you, and you cross state lines, you need an IFTA license and decals from your base state, and you file every quarter. Run only inside your home state and IFTA does not apply. Pickups and personal vehicles under the weight line are out too.

What to gather before you start

Filing goes fast when your numbers are ready. You need three things for the quarter:

  • Total miles by state. Every mile you ran, split by jurisdiction. Loaded, empty, and personal miles all count as distance traveled.
  • Gallons of fuel bought by state. Backed by receipts. The receipt has to show the date, the seller, the location, the number of gallons, and the price.
  • The tax rate for the quarter. Each state sets its own diesel rate, and rates can change from one quarter to the next. Use the rates for the quarter you are filing, not last quarter and not next.

How to split your miles by state

This is the piece that takes the most work, and it is the piece auditors care about most. You need real miles for every jurisdiction you touched, and every mile counts: loaded, empty, deadhead, and personal use all go in as distance traveled. There is no such thing as a mile that does not belong to some state.

You can capture the split three ways. GPS or an ELD does it automatically and is the cleanest, because the record is made as you drive. A trip sheet works if you note your odometer at each state line and each stop. Mapping software can rebuild a route, but reconstructed miles are weaker than miles you logged live. Whatever method you pick, use the same one every quarter so your numbers are consistent from one return to the next.

One more thing people forget: a state you only clipped for twenty miles still goes on the return. If your wheels turned there, it counts, and leaving it off is the kind of gap that gets noticed.

The math, the way the return does it

Here is the part people trip on. IFTA taxes fuel based on where you burned it, not where you bought it. So the form works out how much fuel you used in each state, then credits you for the tax already baked into the fuel you bought there. Four steps:

  • Fleet MPG = total miles divided by total gallons bought, across everywhere.
  • Taxable gallons for a state = that state's miles divided by your fleet MPG. That is the fuel you burned there.
  • Net taxable gallons = taxable gallons minus the gallons you actually bought in that state. Positive means you owe. Negative means you paid too much there and get a credit.
  • Tax = net taxable gallons times that state's rate. Add it all up across every state for your bottom line.

Two states, Kentucky and Virginia, tack on a surcharge. The surcharge is figured on the gallons you burned there, with no credit for buying fuel in that state. So you pay it on the miles no matter what. Indiana used to have a surcharge too, but for 2026 it folded that into its base diesel rate, so there is no separate Indiana surcharge line anymore.

Worked example

Say the quarter looks like this: 2,000 miles in Michigan and 1,000 in Ohio, and you bought 400 gallons in Michigan and 100 in Ohio.

Total miles 3,000, total gallons 500, so fleet MPG is 6.0.

Michigan taxable gallons are 2,000 divided by 6, which is 333.33. You bought 400, so you overpaid there and earn a credit of $34.93. Ohio taxable gallons are 166.67, you only bought 100, so you owe $31.33. Net it out and you land at a credit of $3.60 for the quarter.

Filing with your base jurisdiction

You file one return with your base state, usually through its online tax portal. The return covers every member jurisdiction on a single form. If your states net out to money owed, you pay your base state and it settles up with the others. If you net to a credit, you can carry it or request a refund, depending on your state's rules.

File on time even if you owe nothing, and keep a copy of everything you submitted. That copy is your best friend if a question ever comes up later.

2026 deadlines, including the weekend shifts

IFTA is quarterly. Each return is due the last day of the month after the quarter closes. When that date lands on a weekend or holiday, it rolls to the next business day. For 2026 that means two of the four move:

  • Q1 (Jan to Mar): due April 30, 2026.
  • Q2 (Apr to Jun): due July 31, 2026.
  • Q3 (Jul to Sep): due November 2, 2026, because October 31 is a Saturday.
  • Q4 (Oct to Dec): due February 2, 2027, because January 31 is a Saturday.

The zero-report rule

This one catches good drivers. As long as your IFTA license is active, you file every quarter, even a quarter where you ran no miles and bought no fuel. That is called a zero report. Skipping it because there was nothing to report is exactly how people rack up penalties and get their license flagged. If you are licensed, you file. Every quarter. No exceptions.

A few slip-ups that cost people

Most IFTA trouble is not fraud. It is small, avoidable errors. Watch for these:

  • Wrong quarter's rates. Rates move. Filing this quarter with last quarter's chart makes every state's tax a little off.
  • Rounded miles. Clean hundreds on every state scream "estimate." Report the real numbers your records show.
  • A dropped state. Forgetting a jurisdiction you only passed through leaves a hole between your fuel stops that does not add up.
  • Thin receipts. A fuel receipt missing the date, location, or gallons may not count as proof, so keep the full ticket.
  • Filing late. Even a return that owes nothing gets a penalty if it is late. Put the deadlines in your phone.

None of these are hard to avoid once you know them. Keep clean records as you go, use current rates, and file on time, and the quarter closes without drama.

That is the whole loop

Gather your miles and gallons, pull the right rates, run the four steps, file with your base state by the deadline, and keep your copies. Do that four times a year and you are square.
